# Bumpy road to a new Agentic Paradigm
When discovering new things, like I have been doing with Agentic Software Development, it is always the most fun to share how awsome something is. But scaling up the process has not been all smooth sailing. Even as I was writing my previous post I knew that there were some yet undiscovered challenges up ahead. My previous blog post described three stages of using AI in development, a basic maturity model as you could call it. 
Currently I would still place my development process at the second level of the maturity model. The first application of these new development methods was on a relatively small backlog item. Now I am figuring out how to best apply this to much larger project that spans multiple sprints and many larger backlog items.
At the same time there have been many interesting discussions with co-workers on the subject that have given me new insights. In this blog post I am going to dive into the challenges faced, critisisms recieved and new insights gained. 

## Context cluttering
Context is at the center of enabeling a fully agentic development process, but there is a sweetspot to be found of providing enough context, but not too much. A context that contains too much information will lead to your agent forgetting details or missing the bigger picture. I have found this to be the most fundamental challenge. Most of the other challenges directly contribute to a degraded context.

## Too descriptive
In the Multiverse development cycle I described in my previous post, this context is built by the different phases (or perspectives as I called them) of development. These perspectives themselves are also created by agents for the most part. 

Even though I have grown fond of the Claude models for software development, it is sometimes a bit over-eager to create content. More content leads to more clutter, wich makes the agent less capable of managing this context. This has lead to some negative spirals that ended up with an unusable context.

## Review blindness
One of the first challenges I experienced is the incredible difficulty of properly reviewing AI generated text. [research psychological effects to explain this]
There have been cases where I have looked at a set of acceptance criteria over and over again and thought "Looks good to me", only to immediatly think it does not make sense as soon as it is discussed with team members.
However, that last example also immediatly provides a valid solution to this problem. Having to explain something makes you actually think about some concept, instead of just reading the text. For some this reviewing might come easier than for others, but for me on my own it's a bit of a blind spot.
