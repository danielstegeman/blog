For decades, the software development lifecycle has followed a well-established, linear path. Whether using waterfall, agile, or even DevOps-inspired approaches, the process typically moves step by step: requirements are gathered, designs are created, code is implemented, tests are written and executed, and finally, the product is deployed and maintained. Each phase is expected to build on the previous one, with clear boundaries and handoffs between roles and responsibilities.

This linearity made sense in a world where each transition required significant coordination and context switching. Moving from design to implementation, or from implementation to testing, often meant pausing to document, communicate, and realign teams. The result was a predictable, structured workflow—reliable, but sometimes rigid and slow to adapt to change.

However, in practice this proces is sometimes far from perfect. How often have you gone through all the steps properly, only to find out during implementation that your analysis and design are technically unfeasable. Or that the acceptance tests produce very unexpected changes?

Today, however, the rise of AI-powered tools and agentic workflows is challenging this fundamental assumption. What if development didn’t have to be a straight line from start to finish? What if, instead, we could move fluidly between any stage, or even work on multiple phases at once?
